*[MUSIC: low, tense drone — no melody, just atmosphere]*
*(Black screen, then slow fade to aerial shot of São Paulo at night)*

It's 1998. São Paulo, Brazil. A city of 20 million people — the largest in South America.

On the surface, it's a city alive with motion. Markets, motorcycles, music, and millions of lives intersecting every day.

But beneath that surface, something terrible was happening.

Young women were disappearing.

[PAUSE]

Not kidnapped. Not taken by force. They were leaving willingly — walking away with a stranger who promised them something they couldn't resist.

A chance to become a model.

[PAUSE]

[SLOW] They never came back.

*(Hard cut to black)*

**[TITLE CARD: BR FILES — "THE PARK MANIAC"]*

## 📁 PART ONE: THE CITY AND THE PARK
*[MUSIC: shifts to slow, melancholic piano — barely audible]*
*(Footage or images of Parque do Estado — daytime, peaceful, green)*

São Paulo's Parque do Estado is one of the largest urban parks in the world. Over four square kilometers of dense Atlantic Forest, right in the middle of one of the most populated cities on Earth.

To most residents, it's a place of peace. Families on weekends. Children flying kites. Couples on benches.

But the park has another side — dense forest, remote trails, and areas so isolated that you could be completely invisible to the rest of the city.

That's exactly what Francisco de Assis Pereira was looking for.

[PAUSE]

Francisco was born on November 29, 1967, in São José do Rio Preto, a city in the interior of São Paulo state. He grew up in a troubled household. Reports from people who knew him describe a childhood marked by trauma — possible abuse, exposure to violence, a disconnect from normal emotional development that would only deepen over time.

As an adult, Francisco moved to the capital. He worked as a motorcycle courier — what Brazilians call a *motoboy*. He was lean, athletic, and by all accounts, charming when he wanted to be.

He was also a competitive inline skater, participating in local championships. On the surface, he looked like any other young man trying to make it in the big city.

[PAUSE]

But Francisco had a fantasy. And by 1997, he had found a way to act on it.

---

## 📁 PART TWO: THE METHOD
*[MUSIC: tension builds slightly]*
*(Images of São Paulo subway stations — Jabaquara, Sé, Ibirapuera Park)*

Francisco's method was terrifyingly simple.

He would approach young women — always alone — in public places. Subway stations. Shopping malls. Public parks. Places where a stranger striking up a conversation doesn't immediately raise alarm.

He had a script.

He would introduce himself as a talent scout. A *caça-talentos*, as they say in Brazil. He told women they had the look — the face, the body, the presence — to work as professional models. He promised paid photo shoots. He promised connections. He promised a way out of ordinary life.

[PAUSE]

[SLOW] And for young women with dreams, struggling in one of the most unequal cities in the world, that promise was magnetic.

He was well-dressed. Confident. He didn't seem dangerous. He had a motorcycle, which in that context suggested he had work, stability.

He would offer to take them somewhere for a test shoot. A quick ride. Just to see how they looked on camera.

That ride ended in the dense forest of Parque do Estado.

[PAUSE]

The investigators who would later analyze his method were disturbed by one thing above all else. Francisco carried no weapons. He used no physical force to get women onto his motorcycle.

He simply talked.

When asked about this during interrogation, Francisco reportedly said — [SLOW] *"Convincing them was easy."*

---

## 📁 PART THREE: THE VICTIMS
*[MUSIC: stops completely — silence]*
*(This section should be narrated slowly and with reverence)*

Before we go further, we need to talk about the women Francisco targeted.

Because they were not footnotes in his story.

They were the story.

[PAUSE]

Francisco had a type. Investigators would later note that all of his victims shared similar features — long hair, curvy figures, dark or mixed complexions, between 18 and 24 years old. Women who were young, ambitious, and vulnerable enough to trust a stranger's compliment.

[PAUSE]

**Raquel** was 23 years old. She worked as a saleswoman and disappeared in January 1998 while looking for a new job. She was the kind of person who believed that if she worked hard enough, things would get better. Her body was found on January 29th, in the forest of Parque do Estado.

[PAUSE]

**Isadora** was 19. She vanished on February 10th, 1998. Her family didn't know where she had gone. Six months later, her remains were found — in an advanced state of decomposition. Francisco had used a blank check from her checkbook to buy a motorcycle helmet, telling the store clerk they were boyfriend and girlfriend.

Her family denied it. They had never heard of him.

[PAUSE]

**Patrícia** was 24. She disappeared on April 17th. She worked as a saleswoman in downtown São Paulo and dreamed of becoming a model. Francisco found her at a subway station, promised her exactly that dream, and drove her into the forest. Her body was found three months later, identified only by her clothes and jewelry left near the remains.

[PAUSE]

**Elizângela** was 21. She left home on May 9th to go to a shopping mall and never came back. Her body was discovered on July 28th — identified through a fingerprint that had been preserved by the moist soil where she lay.

[PAUSE]

**Rosa** was also 21. A telephone operator. She disappeared on May 24th. Francisco encountered her at Ibirapuera Park — one of the most famous parks in São Paulo — and drove her to Parque do Estado. He later confessed to strangling her. Her body was found on July 9th.

[PAUSE]

**Selma** was just 18 years old. A student from the suburb of Cotia. She disappeared on July 3rd, 1998. Her body was found the very next day — and the discovery of Selma's remains would be the moment everything changed.

[PAUSE]

These are six of the women confirmed in Francisco's conviction. He confessed to eleven murders in total. One victim was never identified.

Eleven women. Eleven families. Eleven futures erased.

[PAUSE]

[SLOW] Remember their names.

## 📁 PART FOUR: THE INVESTIGATION BEGINS
*[MUSIC: returns — investigative, methodical pulse]*
*(Images of police crime scene tape, São Paulo police headquarters)*

In late July 1998, everything collapsed at once.

A boy had gone into the forest of Parque do Estado to retrieve a kite that had fallen. What he found instead would trigger the largest manhunt in São Paulo's history.

A body.

Then another.

Then two more — all discovered within the span of two days.

[PAUSE]

Four bodies. All women. All nude. All face down. All bearing signs of strangulation. All showing evidence of sexual assault.

The city of São Paulo erupted.

The police, led by investigators from the DHPP — the Department of Homicides and Personal Protection — immediately recognized they were not dealing with isolated crimes. The method was too consistent. The location too specific. The victim profile too similar.

[SLOW] They had a serial killer.

[PAUSE]

For most Brazilian investigators in 1998, the concept of a *serial killer* was still relatively new. The lead prosecutor on the case, Edilson Mougenot Bonfim, would later recall having to explain to the jury what a serial killer actually was — a term so unfamiliar in Brazil at the time that he had to study international literature and foreign case files to build his approach.

What the investigators did have were survivors.

[PAUSE]

Not all of Francisco's attacks ended in death. Nine women survived their encounters with him. And as police began to connect the crimes, they reached out to those survivors — women who had been attacked but managed to escape, or who had agreed to go with the man on a motorcycle and something had gone wrong with his plan.

One by one, the survivors described the same man.

[PAUSE]

Then came the break.

A woman called the police. She had been approached by a man fitting the description — at the Sé subway station in central São Paulo. She had refused him. But he had given her a phone number. Written on a piece of paper.

With the name *Jean*.

[PAUSE]

Investigators traced the number to a motorcycle courier company. When they arrived, the man called Jean was nowhere to be found. But the company owner mentioned something strange — one of his employees had recently vanished without explanation. Left a letter and a newspaper behind.

His name was Francisco de Assis Pereira.

[PAUSE]

In his locker at the company, police found something that made their blood run cold.

The ID card of one of the murdered women.

---

## 📁 PART FIVE: THE MANHUNT
*[MUSIC: urgency — faster tempo]*
*(Map of Brazil — São Paulo to Rio Grande do Sul)*

Francisco had fled.

With the police closing in, he had abandoned his job, his city, and everything he knew — and driven south. All the way to the state of Rio Grande do Sul, near the border with Argentina.

[PAUSE]

Police released his photo. The press ran with it. Francisco de Assis Pereira became, overnight, the most wanted man in Brazil.

The country mobilized. TV programs, newspapers, radio stations — everyone was broadcasting his face. A nation of 160 million people was looking for one man.

[PAUSE]

[SLOW] For 23 days, he wasn't found.

[PAUSE]

Then, in a small town in Rio Grande do Sul, a man running a family guesthouse grew suspicious of a new guest who had arrived looking for a room. The guest was nervous. He paid in cash. He gave vague answers about where he was from and what he was doing.

The guesthouse owner had seen the news.

He made a phone call.

[PAUSE]

On August 5th, 1998, Francisco de Assis Pereira was arrested.

When officers arrived, he was calm. Almost eerily so.

He denied everything.

[PAUSE]

But when he was transferred back to São Paulo, reality set in. Survivors identified him immediately. Physical evidence connected him to the victims. And eventually — Francisco confessed.

Not just to seven murders.

To eleven.

And twenty-three attacks in total.

[PAUSE]

He was transferred to the Casa de Custódia de Taubaté — one of Brazil's most secure prisons. When the transport vehicle left the DHPP building, a crowd of more than two hundred people was waiting outside. They screamed. They surged forward. *"Assassino. Lincha. Vai morrer."*

*Murderer. Lynch him. You will die.*

Police barely kept them back.

## 📁 PART SIX: THE MIND OF A KILLER
*[MUSIC: quieter — psychological, unsettling)*
*(Close-up images of case files, psychological reports)*

Who was Francisco de Assis Pereira?

That question occupied psychiatrists, prosecutors, and journalists for years.

[PAUSE]

Forensic psychiatric evaluations diagnosed him with antisocial personality disorder. In the courtroom, prosecutor Bonfim fought hard against the word *maníaco* — maniac — arguing that the label falsely implied a mental illness that could excuse Francisco's crimes. Antisocial personality disorder, he argued, did not exempt someone from criminal responsibility under Brazilian law.

The jury agreed.

[PAUSE]

Francisco was not a disorganized killer acting on blind impulse. He was methodical. He had a consistent approach, a consistent victim profile, a consistent location. He planned. He charmed. He manipulated.

Forensic psychologist Guido Palomba, in the documentary *Investigação Criminal*, explained that Francisco fit the classic profile of what criminologists call an organized serial killer — someone who selects victims deliberately, controls the environment of the attack, and takes steps to avoid detection.

[PAUSE]

During interrogation, Francisco made a statement that stayed with investigators for decades.

When asked how he convinced women to get on his motorcycle and follow him into the forest — without weapons, without threats, with just words — he looked at the detective and said:

[SLOW] *"Qual o ser humano que não tem uma lábia?"*

*"What human being doesn't have a way with words?"*

[PAUSE]

There was something else. Something investigators described only reluctantly in initial reports, but that Francisco himself confirmed.

He would return to the bodies.

After killing his victims, Francisco sometimes came back to the location the next day. He would stay for hours.

He remained until decomposition made it impossible to stay.

[PAUSE]

The clinical word for what Francisco described is *necrophilia*.

[SLOW] He was not, investigators concluded, a man who killed in a moment of explosive rage. He was someone for whom the killing was only the beginning.

---

## 📁 PART SEVEN: THE TRIAL AND THE SENTENCE
*[MUSIC: formal, judicial — slow tempo]*
*(Images of Brazilian courtroom, scales of justice)*

The trial of Francisco de Assis Pereira was a landmark moment in Brazilian legal history.

Prosecutor Bonfim had to educate an entire jury — and through the press, an entire nation — about what serial killers are, how they think, and why the law must treat them differently.

[PAUSE]

Francisco was convicted of homicide, rape, sexual assault, fraud, and concealment of a corpse.

The sentence: [SLOW] two hundred and eighty-five years in prison.

[PAUSE]

But here's where the story takes a turn that has kept Brazilians awake at night ever since.

Brazilian law, as written at the time Francisco committed his crimes, capped the maximum time any person could spend incarcerated at thirty years.

Two hundred and eighty-five years.

Reduced to thirty.

[PAUSE]

Francisco de Assis Pereira is expected to be released in August 2028.

He is currently 56 years old.

[PAUSE]

Prosecutor Bonfim, in interviews decades after the trial, has been direct: *"His danger is not contained by his personality changing. It is contained only by the walls of that prison. The day those walls are no longer there — the risk returns."*

Multiple psychiatrists have concluded that Francisco's condition is irreversible. He is not someone who can be rehabilitated in the traditional sense.

[PAUSE]

[SLOW] And yet, under Brazilian law, the door may open.

---

## 📁 PART EIGHT: THE STRANGE AFTERMATH
*[MUSIC: shifts to something almost haunting — dissonant)*
*(Images of prison mail, letters)*

What happened after Francisco's arrest reveals something deeply uncomfortable about human psychology.

Within weeks of his imprisonment, Francisco began receiving letters.

Not hate mail.

Love letters.

[PAUSE]

In the first month alone, he reportedly received close to one thousand letters from women expressing admiration, attraction, and affection. The phenomenon became so well-known that a journalist wrote an entire book about it — titled *Loucas de Amor*, or *Crazy in Love* — attempting to understand why women were drawn to a convicted serial killer.

[PAUSE]

Francisco eventually married one of his admirers while in prison. The marriage later ended. His former partner described his behavior as deeply strange and disturbing upon closer contact.

The phenomenon has a clinical name: *hybristophilia* — a sexual attraction to people who have committed violent crimes. It is more common than most people realize, and Francisco's case became one of the most prominent examples in Brazilian history.

[PAUSE]

In December 2000, false reports circulated that Francisco had been killed in a prison riot. News outlets ran the story. Hours later, it was retracted.

He was alive.

He remains alive today.

## 📁 PART NINE: THE LEGACY
*[MUSIC: reflective, slow]*
*(Return to images of Parque do Estado — now with a different emotional weight)*

The case of Francisco de Assis Pereira — the Maníaco do Parque — changed Brazil.

It changed how the country talked about serial killers. It changed how prosecutors approached cases of organized sexual violence. It put forensic psychology on the national map. It forced a conversation about the limits of the Brazilian penal code.

[PAUSE]

In 2024, a film was released on Amazon Prime Video in Brazil, dramatizing the case. A documentary series followed. A new generation of Brazilians — many of whom were not yet born in 1998 — encountered the story for the first time.

And the debate about his impending release reignited.

Petitions were filed. Psychiatrists testified before the courts. The Ministry of Public Affairs requested new mental health evaluations to determine whether Francisco could be kept incarcerated beyond 2028.

[PAUSE]

As of today, no final decision has been made.

[SLOW] A man convicted of killing eleven women — and confessing to it — may walk free within the next few years.

[PAUSE]

The women he killed will not.

[PAUSE]

Raquel. Isadora. Patrícia. Elizângela. Rosa. Selma.

And five others, some of whose names we may never fully know.

This is what true crime really is — not a genre, not entertainment, not a puzzle to be solved. It's the permanent absence of someone who should still be here.
